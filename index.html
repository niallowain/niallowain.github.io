Autumn 2020 Question 1(d)

The current share price of a company is €50. The share price has volatility 20%. The risk-free interest rate is 5% per annum. Use the Black-Scholes Formula to compute the price of a European call option on the stock with strike price €53 that expires in three months’ time.

The Black-Scholes Formula for a European Call Option on an asset whose price at time \(t\) is\( S(t)\)

$$(C(t,S(t)) = S(t)\Phi(d_1) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(d2)$$

Where \(K\) is the strike price, \(r\) is the continuously compounded risk-free interest rate, \(\sigma\) is the volatility of the asset,

$$d_1 = {log(S(t)/K)+(r+{1 \over 2}\sigma^2)(T-t) \over \sigma \sqrt{T-t}}$$

\(d_2 = d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T-t}\) and \(\Phi\) is the cumulative distribution function for the standard normal distribution

Figures for this question

$$\sigma = 0.2$$

$$S(0) = €50$$

$$K = €53$$

$$T-t = {3 \over 12}$$

So for this question, \(d_1\) =

$${ln({50 \over 53})+(0.05+{1 \over 2}0.2^2)({3 \over 12}) \over 0.2\sqrt{3 \over 12}}$$

$$= -0.407689$$

This subsequently means...

$$d_2 = -0.407689 - 0.2\sqrt{3 \over 12} = -0.507689 $$

We Return now to \((C(t,S(t)) = S(t)\Phi(d_1) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(d2)\)

$$= 50\Phi(-0.40769) - 53e^{-0.05({3 \over 12})}\Phi(-0.506789)$$

Using Log Tables

$$=50(0.3409) -53(0.9876)(0.3050)$$

$$=€1.08$$

Autumn 2020 Question 2 (b)

Prove the put-call parity formula \( S(t) + P (t) − C(t) = Ke^{ −r(T −t)}\) where r is the risk-free interest rate, and where \( S(t), P (t), C(t)\) are the time t prices of the stock, the put option, and the call option, respectively.

This is Theorem 7.1 in the book

For a stock that pays no dividends the following relation holds between the prices of European call and put options, both with exercise price X and exercise time T :

$$C^E - P^E = S(0) - Xe^{-rT}$$

Proof

Suppose that

$$C^E - P^E > S(0) - Xe^{-rT}$$

In this case an arbitrage strategy can be constructed as follows: At time 0

  1. Buy one share for S(0)
  2. Buy one put option for \(P^E\)
  3. Write and sell one call option for \(C^E\)
  4. Invest the sum \(C^E-P^E-S(0)\) (or borrow, if negative) on the money market at the interest rate \(r\).

The balance of these transactions is 0. Then, at time \(T\)

  1. Close out the money market position, collecting (or paying, if negative) the sum \((C^E-P^E-S(0))e^{rT}\)
  2. Sell the share for X either by exercising the put if \(S(T)\leq X\) or settling the short position in calls if \(S(T) > X\).

The balance will be \((C^E-P^E-S(0))e^{rT} + X\), which is positive from what we have supposed at proof start, Contradicting the No-Arbitrage Principle

Now suppose that

$$C^E - P^E < S(0) - Xe^{-rT}$$

Then the following reverse strategy will result in arbitrage: At time 0

  1. Sell short one share for S(0)
  2. Write and sell a put option for \(P^E\)
  3. Buy one call option for \(C^E\)
  4. Invest the sum \(S(0)-C^E+P^E\) (or borrow, if negative) on the money market at the interest rate \(r\).

The balance of these transaction is 0, at time \(T\)

  1. Close out the money market position, collecting (or paying, if negative) the sum (\(S(0) - C^E + P^E)e^{rT}\)
  2. Buy one share for \(X\) either by exercising the call is \(S(T) > X\) or setting the short psoition in puts if \(S(T) \leq X \) and close the short position in stock.
The balance will be \((S(0) - C^E + P^E)e^{rT}-X\), positive for this supposition, once again contradicting the No-Arbitrage Principle
